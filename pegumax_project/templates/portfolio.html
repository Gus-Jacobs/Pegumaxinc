I'm a Computer Science student and developer who likes building things that are equal parts secure, useful, and a little bit ambitious. My path into software didn't start in a classroom — it started with curiosity, a willingness to break things, and a habit of not stopping until they work.

In a brand-new OOP course in high school, I found a bug baked into the code for the final exam itself. Rather than just work around it, I traced the fault, documented it, and sent my instructor a corrected version. That instinct — dig until you understand it, then make it better — is still how I work.

Between graduating high school and starting college, I didn't coast. I spent that gap stacking certifications and sharpening fundamentals across web development, data, AI, and security — turning downtime into a head start. By the time I walked into my first university class, I'd already built a foundation most students were just beginning.

Today I'm pursuing that drive formally: a B.S. in Computer Science, minors in Mathematics and Applied Artificial Intelligence, and certifications in Cybersecurity and Software Development. The goal isn't just a degree — it's becoming the kind of engineer who can design a system, secure it, and make it intelligent.

In 2026 I took that further and founded Pegumax LLC, a software company built around shipping genuinely useful tools and apps — kept free wherever it's feasible. Running it end-to-end has taught me the parts of engineering that don't show up in a classroom: CI/CD pipelines, real deployment, managing separate dev and production environments, and the operational discipline of keeping live software healthy. It's where I learned to think like an owner, not just a coder.
